This single vehicle crash occurred at an intersection without traffic signals. The road is a 2-lane undivided (two-way traffic) downhill asphalt roadway. It runs from east to west and leads into an open apartment complex (not gated). It is a two-lane asphalt roadway from south to north. The speed limit is 48 kph (30 mph) for both roadways. The road surface was dry and there were no detrimental atmospheric conditions at the time of the crash. The crash happened during early afternoon hours on a weekend and there was little traffic on the roadways.

The only vehicle (V1), a 2004 Ford Explorer with two occupants, was traveling west in the first lane of traffic. An unidentified passenger vehicle crossed through the intersection in front of V1. The driver of V1 intended to apply her brakes but hit the accelerator instead. V1 accelerated through the intersection and departed the roadway to the right, leaving the roadway at the northwest corner. The front of V1 impacted a brick column (height 140 cm/55 inches). V1 drove onto the fallen column where the vehicle came to rest. The vehicle was towed from the scene. Police Reported Travel Speed is Not reported for V1.

A 48-year old female drove V1. Neither the driver nor the right front occupant spoke English. The right front occupant was partially interviewed with the assistance of a Spanish speaking police officer on site. The driver refused to be interviewed and did not want anyone provide any personal information. The right front occupant, the driver's spouse, told the researcher he and his wife were coming from their residence located about 48 km (30 miles) out of town. They were on their way to visit a friend living at the attached apartment complex. When a passenger car passed in front of them, his wife hit the accelerator instead of the brakes. The driver is in good health, does not take any medication and is familiar with her vehicle.

The critical pre crash event was coded "this vehicle traveling - off the edge of the road to the right." The critical reason for the critical event was coded "poor directional control."
