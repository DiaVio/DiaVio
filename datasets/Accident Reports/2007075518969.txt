This three-vehicle crash occurred before a four-leg, traffic signal-controlled intersection. On the south leg of the intersection, the roadway is divided by a painted median, and the northbound traffic lanes consist of a right turn lane, two through lanes, and a left-turn lane. This roadway is straight, uphill in the northbound direction, and has a posted speed limit of 64 kmph (40 mph). This crash occurred in the early afternoon on a weekday with heavy traffic flow and congestion.

Vehicle one (V1), a 1987 BMW 325 with one occupant, was traveling northbound in lane two, approaching the intersection. Vehicle two (V2), a 2000 Nissan Pathfinder with two occupants, was stopped at the intersection in lane two, in front of V1. Vehicle three (V3), a 2006 Jeep Grand Cherokee with one occupant, was stopped at the intersection in lane two, in front of V2. The driver of V1 looked down at his glasses inside his car and when he looked up again, traffic had come to a stop at the intersection. The driver of V1 applied the brakes, but not in time to avoid a collision, and the front of V1 impacted the rear of V2. V1 under-rode V2 and pushed it forward and the front of V2 contacted the rear of V3. All three vehicles came to final rest near the points of impact, facing north in lane two. V1 and V2 were towed due to damage. Police Reported Travel Speed is 64 kmph for V1 and 64 kmph for V2.

The driver of V1, a fifty-one-year-old male, was driving from his home to get his glasses repaired. He was well rested, very comfortable with his vehicle, and drives this route daily. He is in good health and takes no medications. He is near-sighted and was not wearing his glasses at the time because they needed to be repaired. He denied having any vision restriction on his license.

The critical pre-crash event for V1 was coded: other motor vehicle in lane, other vehicle stopped. The critical reason for the critical event for V1 was coded as a driver related factor: internal distraction. Inadequate surveillance, vision impairment, and traffic congestion were coded as associative factors for V1.

The driver of V2, a twenty-one-year-old female, was driving from a store and going to another store. She was well rested, very comfortable with her vehicle, and drives this route daily. She is in good health and takes no medications.

The critical pre-crash event for V2 was coded: other motor vehicle in lane, traveling in same direction with higher speed. The critical reason for the critical event was not coded to this vehicle. Traffic congestion was coded as an associative factor.

The driver of V3, a fifty-year-old male, was on an errand driving from his home to a store. He was well rested, very comfortable with his vehicle, and drives this route several times a month. He is in good health and takes vitamins on a regular basis.

V3 was not involved first harmful event. The critical reason was not coded to this vehicle. Traffic congestion was coded as an associative factor.
